Abstract

When faced with the decision of an invasive surgery versus modifying medical devices, physicians often choose to modify medical devices in off-label, or unapproved, ways. Due to this off-label use, companies, hospitals, and physicians face many legal liabilities and risks. To lower liability risks, physicians are able to submit a Physician Sponsored Investigational Device Exemption (PSIDE) for FDA approval. However, creating a PSIDE increases the physicians workload with what information needs to be supplied and approved. Creating a way to reduce barriers around PSIDEs is pertinent to allow for more immediate medical operation when there are no devices in a certain field. In order to find a way to make it easier for physicians to apply for PSIDE approval, I will be researching what companies can supply physicians for PSIDE approval on an individual basis and what consistent information can be provided overall for Investigational Device Exemption (IDE) approval. Doing research through W.L. Gore will allow me to create a meaningful solution in part with a company that supplies numerous medical devices. I am hoping to lower individual PSIDE requirements by creating a template on what information can consistently be provided and what companies can supply for individual cases while gaining experience working with W.L. Gore.

Hello!

My name is Avery, and I am a mountain biking, basketball playing, and ballet dancing senior at BASIS Flagstaff. For my research project, I will be taking a closer look at how companies can help lower physician requirements and workload around Physician Sponsored Investigational Device Exemptions (PSIDEs).

What can we do when we need a medical device that does not yet exist?

Well, there is the option of open surgery, increasing infection rate and recovery time. Or we can just wait to see how the problem develops. However, if there is a way to modify a current device to allow for the best outcome possible, many doctors will take it.

This creates a separate issue: off-label use. Off-label use is unapproved use of any medical device or drug, but this is an incredibly broad term. Off-label use can include anything from a device slightly off size approval or a device that has a hole burned in it and is drastically altered. With off-label use, the physician subjects themselves, the hospital, and the device company to legal risk if something were to go wrong.

Although this issue may seem like it has no way to be solved, physicians can take another route with PSIDEs. A PSIDE allows the physician to modify the medical device in an approved and monitored way while decreasing the legal risk of all the parties involved.

However, PSIDE creation takes a great deal of work and time for the physician, so many physicians will opt for just off-label use. This is where my senior project steps in.

To reduce the legal risk of all parties involved, companies, such as W.L. Gore (Gore), are interested in physicians utilizing devices as PSIDEs instead of being off-label. I will be researching how Gore and other companies are able to help lower individual PSIDE requirements for the physicians by creating a template on what information can consistently be provided and what information companies can supply for individual cases.

  11. Is this a US only thing or would it be easier or more difficult in other regulatory environments?
  12. Why is the FDA turning to more Real World Data (RWD) use?

From our meeting, I learned quite a bit about PSIDEs and IDEs from a regulatory affairs standpoint. There are a couple areas that create barriers for PSIDE creation:

  • Technical Information
    • Physicians, when modifying a company device, often lack the technical information required to submit a PSIDE. This includes testing for the device or other publications to create reasonable assurances for no harm to patients. This is especially an issue when it comes to a device a physician modified themselves, as, in most cases, the benchtop testing has not ever been done, and, even if some has been done, there is often a lack of long-term durability testing data. This is understandable given physicians probably do not have the resources, skills, or time to design and complete the tests.
    • For technical information on the already available device, physicians are able to request a letter from companies to submit to the FDA. This letter allows the FDA to access device master file documents on the medical device that are already on file with the FDA, so the FDA can have all the prior technical information on the device without sharing the device master file with the physician.
  • End Goal
    • There needs to be an end goal with what can happen with the data gathered. Often, the end goal is device approval. However, there needs to be a certain number of follow-up patients with a sufficient length of follow up (example: 1 year follow up after the implant procedure) to allow for a statistical endpoint.

Another notable struggle surrounding PSIDEs is who owns the data and controls data usage on the device. With larger clinical trials, the company owns the data, and, if the device doesn’t work one way, they are able to put away that data for later use. With physicians owning the data, they are able to publish whatever they want surrounding the data they collected. This is worrisome for companies because it can taint the impression of information and put the company in a bad light, even if the physician was not following device protocol.

Bryan also spoke to how off-label use has been viewed at companies like Gore in the past. Off-label use has been a taboo subject that many people would avoid talking about. Many people would not promote or support it. However, more recently, it has become less of a taboo subject.

Week 3: Understanding Device Modification

March 20, 2024 9:18 am

Hello again!

This week I met with Bryan R. to talk about the process of device modification and why it is necessary with Physician Modified Endografts (PMEGs). Device modification is not condoned, but physicians will opt to do it if necessary.

There is always going to be a reason for a physician to modify a device. Usually, it is one of two reasons:

  • The device does not fit the anatomy of the patient for what is being treated. The device may end before the area that needs treated ends. There are many reasons that a device may not fit anatomy. Every anatomy is different, and every patient is unique. For example, there is quite a bit of variation surrounding the distance and location of where vessels begin and end.
  • The physician does not want to cover too many vessels. When using a device that covers more than the necessary area, the branching vessels, including intercostal vessels that lead to the spine, are covered as well, leading to a shortage of blood supply to those areas. If too many vessels are covered, the risk of paraplegia increases. In turn, physicians only want to cover what is needed.

From there, Bryan walked me through the steps of device modification, presenting different fluoro-images and x-rays. Unfortunately, I cannot show them here, but the images presented different cases of device modification and why it was necessary in each case to treat the patients.

When modifying the device, physicians contact the companies for guidance on what can and cannot be done to treat the patient. After discussing the options and deciding on what to alter, the device is set up for modification.

Instead of opening the entire device, the device is opened to the end of what is being modified, secured by a slipknot and needle to stop deployment. Once open, the patient’s CT scan is referenced to decide where to put the modifications.

With PMEGs, there are often two types of modifications:

  • Fenestrations: holes in the devices. Fenestrations must be lined up perfectly in the patient in order to work.
  • Portals: inner branches. Portals can be maneuvered to fit properly in the branches and are easier to move around. These are very universal.

After altering the device, wires are run through each fenestration and portal to align the device within the patient, and the shipping sheath is put back on to shrink down the device.

It was fascinating to meet with Bryan and go in depth on device modification. It definitely displays how device modification and off-label use are necessary at times to treat the patient and why it is done so often. From there, we discussed what Gore grants often support. As it turns out, there are 3 main categories:

  1. Fellowship Grants
  2. Educational Grants
  3. Research Grants

At Gore, the bulk of the grants is educational. From a PSIDE standpoint, physicians are either looking for educational grants or research grants depending on the requested aid. Research grants typically request more aid than educational grants. However, Gore is willing to fully or partially support grant requests depending on what the request is. It is encouraged to ask for the needed amount in a study.

Within grant applications, Gore is typically looking for clinical, scientific, or healthcare topics within the therapeutic areas that Gore supports. They are really looking to individuals or companies demonstrating how to make medicine better, often pairing perfectly with PSIDEs.

When reviewing applications, applications often miss highlighting why Gore should fund the grant. This creates the grant request to be less appealing when reviewing because it can be difficult to support a request when you have to search out the value.

Overall, I am planning on recommending creating and submitting a grant application for a PSIDE in my PSIDE creation and maintenance Power Point. It was an awesome opportunity to dive into how the grants work at Gore.

Week 9

May 1, 2024 9:19 am

Hello everyone!

This week has been pretty eventful as we are coming to a close with our senior projects. On the senior project end of things, I have been working on presentations and had an informal interview with a FDA representative.

I was curious about how views and thoughts surrounding Physician Sponsored Investigational Device Exemptions (PSIDEs) varied based on the role (i.e. physician, regulatory affairs, and FDA), so many of my questions were similar to a lot of my other interviews.

I will begin by going over the FDA’s role in IDEs and PSIDEs.

As a whole, the FDA regulates medical devices, but they do not want to control medicine as a whole. Their goal is to promote patient safety along with medical innovation. PSIDEs and IDEs allow both of these goals to be satisfied, as the FDA is carefully watching the safety for the patients enrolled in the IDE studies while allowing companies or physicians to meet unmet patient needs through the IDE or PSIDE.

The FDA does see different results between PSIDEs and IDEs. Because physicians and their teams do not have as many resources as large medical companies, it is difficult for the FDA to hold the PSIDEs and IDEs to the same standard with topics like device testing. For this reason, many PSIDEs utilize available, appropriate information and physician experience in the subject surrounding their PSIDE.

Because it can be difficult to justify safety for some PSIDEs, the FDA can request more reports and be able to monitor the situation. The FDA is also able to request inspections for documents and outcomes if they feel it is needed. Being under a PSIDE allows for greater patient safety monitoring than a physician operating off-label allows for.

I also asked about what typically does not work with PSIDEs as seen from the FDA’s side. As it turns out, really novel approaches with little or no evidence to support the IDE proposal typically does not allow the PSIDE to move forward. The FDA has no way of being comfortable with novel ideas that have no evidence backing them. Another difficulty often seen is a PSIDE having any major patient safety concerns or reporting concerns. Again, patient safety is a huge part of what the FDA is looking for and clear reporting is necessary to ensure this.

Another necessary component to be clear about is the PSIDE or IDE goal. The purpose of an IDE is to gain device approval and bring the devices to the market. The FDA does not have the resources for a ton of IDEs, so they want a clear purpose for the IDE. A PSIDE is not for the sole purpose of operating off-label.

From there I asked about what the FDA believes what companies are able to provide physicians. A huge component companies are able to provide is technical input about the device along with creating open dialogue between the physicians, FDA, and the company itself.

Overall, the FDA supports the purposeful creation of PSIDEs and IDEs, as they want to promote medical innovation and provide monitoring for patient safety.
